Accomplished Artist Bridging Traditional and Contemporary Practices

Kaiser A. ÖzHun is a multidisciplinary artist and researcher whose practice is shaped by sustained international exhibition experience and rigorous art-historical scholarship. Active since 1994, he has exhibited extensively across Asia and Europe, including China, Hong Kong, Japan, Malaysia, England, and Sweden. His works are represented in professional private art collections.

More...

His academic formation includes classical fine art training in China, an MFA grounded in Renaissance painting techniques, and a Master’s degree in East Asian Art from Sotheby’s Institute of Art. This combined studio and scholarly background underpins a practice that bridges historical visual systems with contemporary methodologies.

ÖzHun’s research centers on Buddhist cave art of the Tarim Basin—particularly Kizil, Khotan, and Turfan—approached as transregional sites of cultural exchange linking Central Asia, China, Korea, and Japan. Working across oil painting, ink, drawing, and AI-assisted processes, he investigates abstraction as a historically continuous visual language.

His geometric oil paintings employ circles, arcs, and angular forms to establish rhythmic structures that balance precision and emotional resonance, proposing abstraction as a space where historical memory and spiritual perception converge.

Classic University Style – Oil Painting

ÖzHun’s training follows a classical university model of painting education, emphasizing Renaissance methodology, including draftsmanship, anatomical study, linear perspective, and chiaroscuro. Traditional oil painting techniques such as underpainting and glazing form the technical foundation of his practice.

Geometric Abstract Modern – Oil Painting

This body of work explores a dialogue between geometric precision and painterly sensibility. Dense oil pigments generate depth and surface variation, allowing crisp structural forms to coexist with subtle, expressive mark-making. The compositions unfold as rhythmic systems of color, form, and spatial tension.

Buddhism Cave Art

This work constitutes a research-based visual interpretation informed by the spiritual and artistic heritage of early Buddhist traditions along the Silk Road. It engages historically with mural painting practices while reframing them through contemporary material and pictorial methods.

The Kizil Caves, situated along the northern bank of the Muzart River in Kösen (ancient Qiuci), Xinjiang, represent some of the earliest and most significant surviving examples of Buddhist cave art in Central Asia.
